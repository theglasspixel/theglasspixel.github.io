Art Marketing 101: The Social Network (Part III)

Part I
Part II


My assistant Clarisse tells me I should go straight to the subject matter as to avoid losing myself in pointless meanderings that neither inform nor entertain, and, she says, may even mislead our esteemed readers and potential art buyers, who, upon getting lost in long paragraphs of barely contained angry, anti-social rants, will surely abandon us to the much more sugary parts of the interwebs.

She also insists that my knowledge of the English language is, at best, risible, and, at worst, offensive to anyone who does know about such things as adjectives, tenses, conjunctions, those dangling things that she tells me are very bad, and that, anyway, she doesn't now have the time to review my texts, and that with her husband and kids now living here (rent-free, I should add) I should really pull my own weight and stop bothering her with all this silliness.

Anyway (I should really stop saying anyway so much), if you haven't read parts I and II of this series, please do so now, as they convey information of the utmost importance for you, me, the entire human race, and beyond.

Alright. Let's continue with our bullet-point advices for pleasing the algorithms and getting some of our fellow human beings to open their wallets in our favor:


Be social: market your art by networking.

I know. I know. This is kind of getting repetitive. But there really is a point to all this nuisance: closing yourself off in the peace and comfort of your own home, where you can be at ease, without fear of being judged, criticized, mocked, lied to, stepped on, and so on, while constantly being asked to buy this, purchase that, is not the best way to having your masterpieces recognized, admired, and—most importantly—exchanged for cold, hard cash which you can then use to buy yourself some food and other such luxuries.

The most reliable method while getting started is to find the whereabouts of successful artists and simply hang around with them. Yes, follow them around to art exhibitions, fairs, cafés, nightclubs, shoeshine parlors, art clubs and organizations, cinema screenings, brothels and sexy parties, political gatherings, bank robberies, manifestations of social contrariness, and so on.

Ask them for advice. Get them to buy you a meal now and then. Sleep on their couch, or, when they refuse to give you shelter, under their porch—if they happen to live off-towers—or out on the veranda—but this can get tricky if they live in the upper floors of the towers.

Take note of all the organizations where said artists get together to pat themselves in the back while drinking ice-cold strawberry daiquiris and complain about the state of the world, how everything is going to shit, how the towers are becoming unlivable, and how the outside world is not-so-slowly being eaten alive by a voracious, vengeful biosphere intent on getting rid of all humans everywhere, and—I guess I'm meandering again... The point is, take note of those organizations and then proceed to make yourself a nuisance and don't stop until they accept you in their midst.

Unfortunately, I don't have the time right now to continue with this exposition of useful advices to getting... What was it really?... Ha! I know: art marketing! That's it. Clarisse is sitting on my shoulder and having a laugh. I guess my humble blog post really is as amusing and entertaining as I planned it to be.

Anyway, I hope I'll soon enough have the necessary time to continue with this list. Until then: don't forget your daily conjuration!

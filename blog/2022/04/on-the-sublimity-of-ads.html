On the Sublimity of Ads

Influenced by Hans Kritopoterous's Artistry of the Sub-Lunar Mental States, Jean Burrous was the most vocal proponent of the idea of Surveillance Art, which states that the person under surveillance *is the work of art*.

Being the proverbial starving-artist, isolated, malnourished, and paranoid, Jean spent years closed off in a tiny room down in the lower floors of one of the older towers. There, he read, according to him, thousands of books about digital art, computers, algorithms, privacy-enhanced surveillance systems, dreamscape advertisement-delivery short-waves, and, naturally, the old books on abstract expression, action painting, color-field theory, etc.

He then emerged from his hole with a fully formed idea: the human being was now in a new stage of evolution. No longer should we strive for happiness, contentment, enjoyment, or even contact with each other.

Instead, we should find the sublime in our connections to the machines. Or, more exactly, to the algorithms. Specifically, those algorithms that only exist to observe and modify our physical and mental activities.

"A camera is an eye from the infinite; an ad, an agent of psychic evolution," Jean wrote in one of his earlier articles.

Advertisement afforded, according to him, a pure, elevated connection between human and mathematics, and it was more desirable than inter-human relationships.

"If we exist, it's just so we can experience the pure joy of commercial streams of consciousness, the holy connection between man and corporation-designed algorithms."

He did, however, despise political propaganda in advertisement form, since it wasn't purely commercial in its intentions.

When other thinkers pointed out that Jean's ideas were just a hodgepodge of conflicting, barely-formed theories trying to hide what was essentially savage, autocratic capitalism, his defenders quickly responded by publishing thousands--maybe millions--of machine-written studies claiming to prove Jean's philosophical theorems down to the subatomic particles.

"It was written in the big-bang," one paper declared. "The ad is the sublime. The mind a mere receptacle for commercial messages."

Image boards were flooded with computer-generated art indistinguishable from advertisement.

This new art was promptly declared the one-true-art. The digital expression of humanity's subconscious.

The ad was god.

The Ad is God.

Long live the Ad.

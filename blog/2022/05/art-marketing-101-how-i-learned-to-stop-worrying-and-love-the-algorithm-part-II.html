Art Marketing 101: How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love the Algorithm (Part II)

Part I


Last night I had a dream. I really shouldn't bother you with it, but I guess I'm going to anyway. You see--the thing is--how should I start... Well, when I woke up today, first thing I saw was this cute little spider perching right on top of my nose. And the dammed thing winked at me!

Actually, it was not that little. Or cute. More like a black grape with hairy legs, and long eyelashes, lots and lots of curvy eyelashes. I really don't know how she can see anything with so many freaking eyelashes.

So I decided to help her out. You know, we should help our fellow living-beings, and all that.

"Can you do anything?" I asked her.

"Sure. Lots of things," she replied. "Why do you ask?"

"I think I might be able to help you."

"I don't need no help."

"Sure you do. All spiders need help."

Anyway, long story short, I hired Clarisse as my personal secretary, and from now on she's going to help me out with all the intricacies of the English language.

As I already said in [Part I] of this series, the road to fame, glory, and artistic achievement is not really a road, but more like a labyrinth. So, imagine yourself as a rat. One of those cute, fluffy rats that scientist love to poke and cut into little pieces and---no, wait... That's not really the metaphor I was looking for...

Clarisse tells me I'm rambling. Alright. I guess I should jump straight to what matters, and continue with my bullet-point advices. (also, don't forget to always start these practices by conjuring the right entities.)


You Should Enter Art Competitions

As all artists know very well, the upper floors of the towers are fiercely protected by all sorts of gatekeeping agents of the nastier sort.

Once in a while, one of our comrades is lucky enough to squeeze in through one of the tunnels of poison ivy, but even then the feast usually lasts for only a short while.

How many of us have gotten up there only to be thrown down an elevator shaft after only a couple of days or even hours?

Thus is the art business.

Anyway, as for art competitions, the rules are usually pretty simple: find a fellow artist residing in the same floor as you and follow them around until they happen to pass by an open elevator shaft. Then simply help them take the plunge.

Rinse and repeat until you're the only artist on the entire floor.

To be frank, this process can take years or even decades, as it is very difficult to find an open elevator shaft.

Also, artists (myself included) seem to very distrustful of elevators, for some reason.


In Person Art Promotion. Do Your Own Art Shows.

I'm going to tell you a little story about my last art show. It was off-city, far deep in forest territory. I had to take two trains, one motor-boat, and two canoes just to get there, as no helicopter pilot wanted to transport me for the money I had available at the time.

The small town was built on top of a swamp, with a pier of rotting wood extending out to the greenish waters. Everything had a disgustingly sugary smell, like orchids mixed with absinthe and decaying bodies.

The town's people all lived up in tree-houses, away from crocodiles, carnivorous plants, and the kind of giant butterflies that just love to grab tiny pink humans from their cribs. You have to be ready to fight for your life at all times, if you want to live I such place.

Seen up-close, the houses look like flickering images, as seen in those ancient TVs with oval-shaped screens and large rotating knobs. The town-folks, on the other hand, look absolutely normal, but sound like out of tune radio stations, complete with advertising in between every other sentence and all.

Clarisse tells me my poor understanding of the language is starting to show a little too much, and that no one will understand what the hell I am talking about.

It doesn't matter. I'm describing things exactly as I remember them, and that's all I really care.

The gallery only had three of my images, by the way, and two of those looked like they had been dropped in the acid swamp waters. I was so pleased with the result that I tried to do the same to the third image. Unfortunately, the gallery owners didn't like my idea and I was forced to spend the night in a shoddy cage suspended above the water, exposed to the nighttime hallucinogenic vapors...

So I woke up back in my apartment in the towers, and the nice lady from the Communal Care Association told me that I had been very lucky indeed.

"Please promise me you'll never try to expose your, hmmm, art again," she said.

I didn't promise a thing, of course.

Three months later, I got a message from my bank saying a very strange thing indeed: there was money in my account!

Apparently, the gallery had sold one of my images. The other two, they said, turned to dust due to the acid.

With the money, I was able to buy a vegetable sandwich and a small bottle of synthetic red wine.

What a day that was!

Oh boy, I'm running out of blog-space again. I guess I'm not going to write all my remaining SEO advices in this part after all...

Also, Clarisse tells me I'm running low on keywords, that the Mighty Algorithm is never going to find me, that maybe I should follow my own SEO recommendations, and that she's bringing her husband and kids to live with us, so I better stock up the fridge 'cuz there's going to be a lot of mouths to feed...
